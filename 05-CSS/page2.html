An Introduction to beat poets

In the 1940s and 50s, a new generation of poets rebelled against the conventions of mainstream American life and writing.
They became known as the Beat Poets––a name that evokes weariness, down-and-outness, the beat under a piece of music,
and beatific spirituality. At first, they organized in New York City, Los Angeles, and San Francisco. By the 1950s,poets at the
heart of the movement had settled in the Bay Area, especially in neighborhoods near Beat poet and publisher Lawrence Ferlinghetti’s
bookstore, City Lights.



By the mid-1950s, the Beats helped to spearhead a cultural vanguard reacting against institutionalized American values, materialism,
and conformity. On October 7, 1955, the Beats gave their first major public poetry reading, a seminal event held at Six Gallery in San Francisco.
Among the five poets to perform their work was Allen Ginsberg, who first read "Howl", a poem in the tradition of Walt Whitman
that Ginsberg described as “an emotional time bomb that would continue exploding… the military-industrial-nationalistic complex.”
